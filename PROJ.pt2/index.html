What To Know About Uranus

Uranus, a large gas- and ice-rich outer planet. Its diameter is less than half that of Saturn.

Uranus is composed mostly of hydrogen and helium, but it has a larger core of ice (including water, methane and ammonia) and rock than either Saturn or Jupiter.

Uranus is unique in that its spin axis is tipped almost 90° from the normal solar system orientation. It seems that Uranus was tipped on its side when it was struck by a large body during its accretion. Moreover, its magnetic field is not centered on the spin axis; Perhaps the magnetic field is generated by convection in the shell of ice.

A system of narrow rings encircles Uranus. The boulder-sized particles are among the darkest objects yet found in the solar system.

Uranus has at least 27 icy satellites. Five are larger than about 500 km in diameter. They are darker and denser than the moons of Saturn. All are heavily cratered, most are fractured perhaps because of the expansion of freezing ice and a few had “young” volcanism.
